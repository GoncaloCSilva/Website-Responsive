Logic

Logic

Sir Robert Bryson Hall II (born January 22, 1990), known professionally as Logic, is an American rapper and record producer. He has released seven studio albums and received two Grammy Award nominations. Logic's first two studio albums — Under Pressure (2014) and The Incredible True Story (2015) — both peaked within the top five on the U.S. Billboard 200 and were certified gold.

No Pressure is the sixth studio album and seventh professional project by Maryland rapper Logic, and serves as a sequel to his 2014 debut, Under Pressure. In a similar fashion to how his 2018 project YSIV was teased, Logic released a “No Pressure Freestyle” in August 2019 that served as the first mention of this album and included lines found in songs on the project. Much like Under Pressure and the album that followed in 2015, The Incredible True Story, No Pressure features the ‘AI assistant’ Thalia in a narrative role similar to that of Under Pressure. Logic returned from a social media hiatus to announce that No Pressure would be released in just over a week and serve as his final project.


NO PRESSURE TRACKLIST


No Pressure Intro

“No Pressure” is the title track and opening song to Logic’s seventh studio album, a sequel to October 2014’s Under Pressure. The intro features Thalia, a familiar voice to Logic fans, as well as a chopped up narration by filmmaker Orson Welles, who Logic uses throughout the album; for example, he also appears on the final track, “Obediently Yours.” Logic dives back into his homelife in Maryland and provides multiple callbacks for fans of Under Pressure. He also speaks on the crack epidemic that plagued the United States in the ‘80s and '90s, and how his own father fell victim to drug addiction. He elaborated on this topic throughout the aforementioned album’s title track, “Under Pressure,” rapping from the perspective of his dad: And on the real, I’m tryin' so hard not to bug you But do you think you could stop rappin' about my drug use? I’m two years clean, no longer a fiend Yeah, I’m 57, but I feel 19


Hit My Line

“Hit My Line” involves Logic preaching to God about the troublesome things that he witness in his day-to-day life. While drawing from previous life experiences during adolescence, he also speaks on his life since becoming famous— a theme that was present on the previous track as well. Logic also speaks on gang violence as well as throwing in a couple of nods to Chicago rapper-producer Kanye West whom he cited as a major influence in his production game on “Perfect.”


GP4

“GP4” finds Logic reminiscing on his come up and how he reached successes despite coming from a low-income background. On the chorus, Logic pays homage to OutKast through interpolation of their 1996 song, “Elevators (Me & You).” This track also serves as the fourth installment in Logic’s Growing Pains series—the last installment was “Growing Pains III” from his debut album, Under Pressure. Interestingly, Logic also sampled OutKast on “Growing Pains II.”


Celebration

On “Celebration,” Logic compares his life growing up to the heights he has reached and celebrates by flexing his status. The song itself is an interpolation of “Celebration” by Kanye West. This isn’t the only track on the album that is a callback to one of West’s songs, as “Heard Em Say” is also an interpolation of Kanye’s song of the same name. He enlists help from Silas — a Maryland rapper he signed to Bobby Boy records in 2019 — who also discusses how his life has changed in just one year under the tutelage of one of his idols.


Open Mic\\Aquarius III

Logic covers familiar topics on this open mic-themed song, such as his rapping skills, family history, and music career. He also uses similar lyrics and samples from his debut album, October 2014’s Under Pressure; including July 1976’s “Do That Stuff” by Parliament, originally found on the aforementioned project’s title track. The second half of this song title refers to the name of the spaceship operated by protagonists Thomas and Khai on Logic’s second studio album, November 2015’s The Incredible True Story. Continuing with the concept of utilizing various elements from his debut, the interlude features a sample which can be heard in the background of the song “I’m Gone.” It also contains part of the the intro from “Warm it up,” off of his seventh mixtape, March 2018’s Bobby Tarantino II.


Soul Food II

“Soul Food II” is the second installment in Logic’s Soul Food series, the first appearing in his October 2014 debut album, Under Pressure. Logic spends the first half of the song speaking on how he has evolved as a person and an artist since the first “Soul Food” dropped, as is evident in his first lines. In the second half, Logic raps about the storyline of his second album, The Incredible True Story, where main protagonists Thomas and Kai are on the quest for the planet Paradise. As explained in Logic’s Twitch livestream listening party for the album, this is how the original “Soul Food” was supposed to sound: "The first time we did it I couldn’t find the person who owned the sample… This song, six years ago, this is how it was supposed to sound. What you guys are listening to right now is not what […] you were supposed to hear the first time".


Perfect

On “Perfect,” the shortest and lone ‘turn-up’ record on No Pressure, Logic switches up to some braggadocios rapping as he speaks on his success in life and as an artist. As mentioned on his Twitch listening party, Logic explains: "I flex, I swag, I do all this other shit, to bring a message in literally the last bar".


man i is

On “man i is,” Logic talks about his youth and how he managed to find his way into rap over a soulful backdrop. He also nods to the artists he listened to as a child and during his come-up, who all helped develop him into the man he is today. Logic’s own in-house producer, 6ix, based the sound around a sample of Tarika Blue’s 1977 record, “Dreamflower,” and lifted the horns from OutKast’s “SpottieOttieDopaliscious” to form the instrumental for this track. He also chopped up Pimp C’s “Knockin' Doorz Down” to create the intro. He previously used this song on 2011’s “Addiction.” It’s worth noting that Tarika Blue’s record was similarly sampled by Erykah Badu on November 2000’s, “Didn’t Cha Know,” which led to legal disputes. In fact, Logic had to ask for Badu’s permission to use the sample, which he also notes on the album’s third track, “GP4”: "I just texted Erykah Badu To let her know what I’m gon' to do Sample “Dreamflower” by Tarika Blue" Interestingly, Logic previously sampled “Didn’t Cha Know” on his 2018 track, “Indica Badu,” as well.


DadBod

“DadBod” is Logic’s way of poking fun at people who want him to go back to his early rap days such as this album’s predecessor, Under Pressure, where he raps about his tough upbringing. Logic explains to the listener that he has evolved past that type of rapping since he no longer experiences these things. He has a new child, a loving wife and his life is generally more mundane now than it was in the past. The term “DadBod” itself is a meme in popular culture, simply being a physical physique that is relatively slim but not strong or toned at all. Logic is symbolically taking on this “average” role for his listeners and his son. A music video was released on July 27, 2020. The video shows him going through his daily life such as walking with his newborn, playing games and hanging out with his wife.


5 Hooks

On “5 Hooks,” Logic talks about how he started from nothing but nonetheless, his first album made the earth shake. The track name is a reflection on how No Pressure contains just five hooks throughout the project. This song samples “Grown Up Calls” by Toro y Moi, which Logic referenced in a phone call during the outro of the previous track, “DadBod.”


Dark Place

On “Dark Place,” Logic opens up about his mental health. From the hate he takes online to feelings that he may never reach the heights listeners put his earlier works on, Logic simply admits that sometimes you simply are sad — and that’s ok. Logic has touched on this subject multiple times in his music before, such as on his April 2017 single “1-800-273-8255,” named after the National Suicide Prevention Lifeline or “Anziety,” where he depicts his personal struggles with anxiety.


A2Z

On “A2Z,” Logic teaches his son — Little Bobby — the alphabet. In Part I, each of his bars throughout the song hit on each letter in the alphabet whether it be direct or subliminal. Logic uses an old, embarrassing recording from 2005 — his first song — in Part II in an attempt to show people what he really started out as.


Heard Em Say

“Heard Em Say” is a motivation track from Logic that seeks to use his story to empower the listener. The song is an ode to Kanye West—one of Logic's greatest influences—as he released a track with Adam Levine in 2005 under the same name.


Amen

“Amen” is about Logic praying for how lucky he has been to have such a loyal fanbase—the RATTPACK—and how lucky Logic has been to be this successful in the rap game.

Obediently Yours

“Obediently Yours” is the No Pressure outro for Logic that samples the August 11, 1946 episode of the “Orson Welles Commentaries,” which is related to Issac Woodard Jr, a black veteran who was beaten and blinded by a white police officer. Given the Black Lives Matter movement in 2020 that swept the world before and continued on after this album’s release, this commentary sample paints a picture that there is still work to be done for each person to be truly equal.
